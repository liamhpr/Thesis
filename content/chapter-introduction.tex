\section{Motivation}
\label{sec:intro:motivation}
Large Language Models (LLMs) have fundamentally transformed the  
landscape of Artificial Intelligence, demonstrating unprecedented
capabilities in natural language understanding, reasoning and 
generation. 
% PROOF:
LLMs are increasingly deployed in critical domains ranging 
from healthcare \parencite{10.1001/jama.2024.21700} 
to legal analysis \parencite{10850911}. 
However, despite their fluency, 
these models suffer from a critical reliability failure:
the tendency to generate plausible-sounding but factual incorrect 
statements, a phenomenon widely known as hallucinations 
\parencite{Huang2023ASO}.

For high-stakes applications, accuracy alone is insufficient. A 
system must also be self-aware of its limitations. A doctor or lawyer 
using an LLM needs to know when the model is uncertain so 
they can verify the output. Unfortunately, modern LLMs are frequently
"confidently wrong", assigning high probability scores to their 
hallucinations due to poor calibration and the nature of their 
training objectives.

Standard uncertainty metrics, such as predictive entropy
\parencite{malinin2021uncertaintyestimationautoregressivestructured}
or lexical similarity 
\parencite{fomicheva2020unsupervisedqualityestimationneural}, 
often fail to detect these errors because they 
rely on a single, precise probability distribution. This 
"point-estimate" approach mathematically conflates two distinct 
sources of uncertainty \parencite{H_llermeier_2021}: 
\textit{aleatoric uncertainty} 
(the natural diversity of valid human language) 
and \textit{epistemic uncertainty} (the model's lack of knowledge). 
When an LLM distributes its 
probability mass across five different ways of saying the correct
answer, standard metrics flag this as high uncertainty, confusing 
benign linguistic flexibility with factual ignorance.

\section{Problem Statement}
\label{sec:intro:problem_statement}
The core problem addressed in this thesis is the unreliability of
single-point probability estimates for detecting hallucinations in
free-form Question Answering.

Current state-of-the-art methods, such as Semantic entropy
\parencite{kuhn2023semanticuncertaintylinguisticinvariances},
attempt to mitigate this by grouping lexically diverse answers
into semantic clusters. However, even Semantic Entropy relies on a
single, static probability distribution generated at a fixed 
temperature. If this underlying distribution is miscalculated,
i.e., peaked around an incorrect fact, the metric will fail to
signal a warning.
To robustly detect hallucinations, we need a framework that moves 
beyond precise probabilities and considers a set of plausible 
model states.

\section{Contributions}
\label{sec:intro:contributions}
This thesis proposes a novel methodology for quantifying epistemic
uncertainty by bridging the gap between \textit{Imprecise Probabilities} 
and Large Language Model decoding.

Drawing on the theory of \textit{Credal Prediction} based on relative 
likelihood \parencite{löhr2025credalpredictionbasedrelative}, we 
introduce a statistically rigorous method to construct a Credal Set 
of semantic distributions. Our specific contributions are as follows: 

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Valid Temperature Selection via Relative Likelihood}:
	We propose a method to identify a statistically valid range of 
	decoding temperatures. By treating temperatures as a 
	hyperparameter and applying an $\alpha$-cut, we systematically 
	discard implausible distributions.

    \item \textbf{Construction of Semantic Credal Sets}:
	We extend the concept of Semantic Entropy to operate over 
	Interval-Valued Probabilities. By computing the minimum and 
	maximum probability of semantic clusters across valid 
	temperatures, we construct a convex credal set that captures
	the model's true epistemic ambiguity.
